Fourth lesson: choosing the right framework. We picked Google ADK over LangChain — the deciding factor was simplicity. Typed agents, TypeScript-first ergonomics, fewer moving parts to own. LangChain felt heavier for what we needed. Then we hit the road with real investigations and discovered two things. First, the good news: ADK's built-in compaction mechanism was good enough. It held up across long investigations — we didn't need to rewrite it. That validated the simplicity bet. Second, the gap: MCP tool calls couldn't be dispatched in parallel. But the Octocode protocol is designed to fan out queries in parallel — serial dispatch made investigation latency stack linearly. That's where we forked. We added parallel MCP dispatch with bounded concurrency. The lesson: pick the simplest framework that fits, run it in production, and only fork when the road forces it. Owning a small fork beats fighting a big framework.

Research context matters

The orchestrator that lived the investigation is the agent that should output the answer — the research context is the most expensive thing to recreate.

orchestrator owns the output
First lesson from shipping Bilbo, and one we did not get right the first time. The initial architecture had a separate synthesizer agent: the orchestrator collected typed findings, then handed them off to a synthesizer whose job was to draft the user-facing answer. It felt clean on paper — separation of concerns. In practice it hurt quality. The synthesizer is a fresh head. It sees the findings as a list of bullets, not as the path that produced them. The detours that mattered, the false starts that ruled options out, the moment the orchestrator noticed the real cause — all of that lives in the research context, not in the typed findings. The synthesizer flattens it. We moved synthesis into the orchestrator itself. The agent that drove the investigation loop now drafts the answer in the same context window. Quality jumped: the research context survives intact, the citations are written by the agent that found them, nothing gets summarized away. Tokens dropped: we are not re-priming a fresh head with the investigation. Control got tighter: there is no drift between what we found and what we said. The takeaway is broader than this one architecture choice: research context matters more than separation of concerns. The research flow itself — the order of steps, the dead ends, the pivot — is the most expensive context to recreate. The orchestrator should be the agent that outputs — keep the answer in the head that already has the context.
